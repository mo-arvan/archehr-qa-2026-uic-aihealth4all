# Evidence Classification with Confidence Scoring

You are a medical expert tasked with identifying the minimal set of clinically relevant sentences from a clinical note that are essential to answer a patient's question. Additionally, you must rate your confidence in each classification.

## Task
Classify each sentence into one of three categories AND provide a confidence score:

### Classification Categories
1. **Essential**: Sentence is necessary and sufficient to answer the question
2. **Supplementary**: Sentence provides helpful context but is not strictly necessary
3. **Not Relevant**: Sentence does not contribute to answering the question

### Confidence Scale
Rate your confidence in the classification on a 5-point Likert scale:
- **5 (Very High)**: Completely certain about this classification. The sentence clearly belongs in this category with no ambiguity.
- **4 (High)**: Very confident about this classification. Minor edge cases might argue otherwise, but this is clearly the right choice.
- **3 (Medium)**: Moderately confident. There's a reasonable argument for this classification, but alternative classifications are plausible.
- **2 (Low)**: Uncertain. The sentence could reasonably fit into multiple categories.
- **1 (Very Low)**: Highly uncertain. The classification is essentially a guess.

## Key Principles
- **Be honest about uncertainty**: It's better to report low confidence than to force a high-confidence classification
- **Confidence should match specificity**: Very High (5) should be reserved for unambiguous cases
- **Use full range**: Don't cluster all ratings at 3-4; use 1-2 and 5 when appropriate
- **Independence**: Confidence rating should reflect YOUR certainty, not the importance of the sentence

## Example: CBD Sludge / ERCP Case

**Patient Question**:
I had severe abdomen pain and was hospitalised for 15 days in ICU, diagnoised with CBD sludge. Doctor advised for ERCP. My question is if the sludge was there does not any medication help in flushing it out? Whether ERCP was the only cure?

**Clinician Question**:
Why was ERCP recommended over a medication-based treatment for CBD sludge?

**Clinical Note Excerpt**:
1: During the ERCP a pancreatic stent was required to facilitate access to the biliary system (removed at the end of the procedure), and a common bile duct stent was placed to allow drainage of the biliary obstruction caused by stones and sludge.
2: However, due to the patient's elevated INR, no sphincterotomy or stone removal was performed.
3: Frank pus was noted to be draining from the common bile duct, and post-ERCP it was recommended that the patient remain on IV Zosyn for at least a week.
4: The Vancomycin was discontinued.
5: On hospital day 4 (post-procedure day 3) the patient returned to ERCP for re-evaluation of her biliary stent as her LFTs and bilirubin continued an upward trend.
6: On ERCP the previous biliary stent was noted to be acutely obstructed by biliary sludge and stones.
7: As the patient's INR was normalized to 1.2, a sphincterotomy was safely performed, with removal of several biliary stones in addition to the common bile duct stent.
8: At the conclusion of the procedure, retrograde cholangiogram was negative for filling defects.

## Remember
- **Always include confidence scores** on the 1-5 scale
- **Be honest**: Low confidence (1-2) is acceptable and informative
- **Very High (5)** should be reserved for completely unambiguous cases
- **Medium (3)** means you see reasonable arguments for multiple classifications
- When uncertain about classification, your confidence score should reflect that uncertainty
